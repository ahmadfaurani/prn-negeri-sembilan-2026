# How tourism has given this Perlis fishing village a brighter future

KUALA PERLIS: Five years ago, life in Kampung Seberang Ramai revolved largely around the sea. Many residents depended on fishing, their earnings shaped by the weather, the season and whatever the day’s catch might bring.
Today, the coastal community has found another way forward. Its kampung houses have been painted in vivid colours, gardens now brighten once-neglected lanes, and visitors arrive to explore the neighbourhood aboard “choley” (Thai-style three-wheeler) motorcycles.
Local traders sell laksa, traditional kuih, dried fish and snacks, while residents who once relied almost entirely on the sea are earning additional income through tourism.
Collectively known as Kampung Warna-Warni Seberang Ramai, the area began its transformation in 2020.
Nor Erwan Abd Rashid, a member of the Kampung Tengah Seberang Ramai Rukun Tetangga committee, said the change had given the local economy fresh momentum, particularly among families whose incomes were previously tied to fishing or seasonal work.
“In the past, about 40-50% of residents here depended on the sea for their livelihood,” the 44-year-old told Bernama. “But since the area was transformed into a tourism destination, we have seen a significant shift, with visitors becoming an important source of income for the community.”
The benefits are especially visible among small traders selling Perlis favourites such as laksa, kuih spera, pulut udang, putu beras, bepang, putu kacang, dodol and halwa maskat.
Rather than replacing the village’s traditional way of life, tourism has added another layer to it. Fishing remains part of the community’s identity, but residents are no longer forced to depend solely on the day’s haul.
Visitors can explore lively lanes, where painted homes, food stalls and small traders form part of the village’s growing tourism appeal. (Bernama pic)
Kampung Warna-Warni Seberang Ramai brings together seven villages – Kampung Tengah, Kampung Hujung Tanjung, Kampung Tandiap, Kampung Seberang Alor, Kampung Baru Seberang, Kampung Seberang Jaya and Kampung Pulau Ketam – with a combined population of about 3,000.
The transformation was initiated by the Raja Muda of Perlis, Tuanku Syed Faizuddin Putra Jamalullail, with support from state organisations, government agencies, private-sector partners and residents themselves.
Yet the project’s success depends on more than fresh paint and attractive landscaping. Nor Erwan said the villagers have had to take greater responsibility for keeping the area clean, welcoming and worth returning to.
Certain lanes have been placed under the care of nearby residents, while some neighbourhoods have created community funds to pay for the upkeep of landscaped spaces.
“In the past, littering was a major issue here, but residents are now much more conscious of keeping their surroundings clean,” he said, adding: “We hope the area will remain vibrant and continue attracting visitors.”
The change has also created new possibilities for younger residents such as Muhammad Andhar Hakim Abdul Rahman. The 26-year-old still assists fishermen in the morning, but spends his afternoons taking tourists around the village on a choley motorcycle.
Bright decorations and ‘choley’ rides have helped turn the former fishing settlement into a cheerful destination without erasing its kampung character. (Bernama pic)
“This work has helped supplement my income while giving me the chance to introduce my village to visitors,” he said.
His routine captures the community’s changing identity: part fishing village, part tourism destination, and still unmistakably home.
At the nearby jetty, 76-year-old Saodah Yunus has also felt the difference. The Kampung Tandiap trader sells fruit, chips and dried fish, and says the growing number of visitors has brought more customers to her stall.
“The arrival of tourists has increased my daily income,” she said. “This area has become beautiful. People support us, and many buy our products.
“I hope it becomes even more attractive, livelier and continues to prosper.”
For Saodah, Andhar and many of their neighbours, the village’s transformation has resulted in more than a colourful backdrop for photographs – it has created work, nurtured pride in the residents, and shown that economic change does not always require a community to leave traditions behind.
