# Why work feels harder than ever, despite productivity tools

KUALA LUMPUR: It’s 9am. Before you’ve replied to a single email, you’ve already logged into Gmail, Microsoft Teams, WhatsApp Web, your HR portal, a project management platform and several shared drives.
Technology was supposed to simplify office life. Instead, for many employees, the workday begins with navigating a growing maze of apps, dashboards and logins before the real work even starts.
A recent survey commissioned by workplace software company Kintone suggests many office workers are feeling exactly that frustration.
Among 506 professionals surveyed in the KL Eco City precinct here, 41.1% said too much manual work was the biggest drag on their productivity.
Another 29.8% pointed to overly complicated systems, while 16.2% struggled with poor visibility across information and projects. A further 12.8% felt they were simply using too many different tools.
While limited in scope, the findings echo a complaint increasingly heard in workplaces worldwide: employees aren’t necessarily overwhelmed by the amount of work they have, but by the number of systems they must navigate to get it done.
Digital overload
Over the past decade, businesses have embraced digital transformation at an unprecedented pace.
Email gave way to instant messaging, shared drives became cloud platforms, and everything from project management and HR to leave applications and customer databases moved online.
Each tool promised to save time. Collectively, however, they tend to leave employees constantly switching between applications, searching for files, copying information between systems, and chasing updates spread across multiple platforms.
“It’s ironic,” said Tsubasa Nakazawa, managing director of Kintone Southeast Asia. “Despite years of digitalisation, many teams feel slowed down by the very tools that were meant to speed them up.”
Workplace experts increasingly describe this phenomenon as “digital overload” or “software sprawl” – layer upon layer of specialised software that rarely works seamlessly together.
The best workplace technology often performs quietly in the background, helping employees focus on their work instead of the software itself. (Envato Elements pic)
For smaller businesses in particular, every new application often means another subscription, another login and another process to learn. The result is a workday frustratingly filled with small interruptions.
Individually, tasks such as searching for documents or copying figures from one spreadsheet into another may take only minutes. Across an entire day, however, they can consume hours.
It’s one reason many employees end the day feeling constantly busy, even if they haven’t completed the work they intended to.
Notably, the findings reflect a broader shift in how organisations are beginning to think about workplace technology.
Rather than measuring digital maturity by the number of tools they have, many companies are asking whether such systems actually work well together – and whether they’re making employees’ jobs easier or more complicated.
Sometimes, improving productivity isn’t about adding another app. It may simply mean removing unnecessary steps, reducing duplication, and giving employees a clearer view of the work in front of them.
After all, the best workplace technology should be almost invisible. When it’s doing its job well, people spend less time managing software – and more time doing the work that matters.
