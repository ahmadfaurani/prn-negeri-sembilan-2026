# Wawasan guns for crucial police vote through retired senior officers

Former Bukit Aman internal security chief Hazani Ghazali (left) and ex-Melaka deputy police chief Razali Abu Samah are two retired police officers who have joined Hamzah Zainudin’s Wawasan.
PETALING JAYA: Parti Wawasan Negara appears to be looking to dominate the votes of policemen and their spouses through the entrance of retired senior officers, including a former Bukit Aman director, say political analysts.
Azmi Hassan.
Akademi Nusantara’s Azmi Hassan and Mazlan Ali of Universiti Teknologi Malaysia said Wawasan president Hamzah Zainudin would have made use of his relationship with these officers to woo them into joining his party.
After all, Hamzah was home minister from March 2020 to November 2022, they said.
Mazlan pointed out that there were over 130,000 policemen across the country, while many had wives and even children aged 18 and above, making them eligible to vote.
While spread out across federal and state constituencies, he said, they still represent a significant chunk of votes, especially in marginal seats where just a hundred votes could change the election results.
Mazlan Ali.
“Just the policemen and their wives alone would come up to more than 200,000 votes. Don’t forget about retired cops. So, when former senior police officers join political parties, they would be able to attract this segment of voters.
“And policemen often have a sense of loyalty and consistency, even when it comes to voting. They’re easier to ‘manage’ compared with regular voters,” he told FMT.
Former Bukit Aman internal security director Hazani Ghazali is a member of Wawasan’s central leadership, while former Melaka deputy police chief Razali Abu Samah is among the candidates fielded by the party in the Negeri Sembilan state election.
Hazani, who was also the first director-general of the Border Control and Protection Agency, also led the Sabah police contingent from 2020 to 2022 as well as the Eastern Sabah Security Command (Esscom) from 2017.
Nothing wrong with ex-cops joining politics
Azmi and Mazlan said there was nothing wrong with retired policemen entering politics and joining political parties since they have already left the force, pointing out that ex-armed forces generals have also done the same.
This includes former armed forces chief Raja Affandi Raja Noor, who contested as a Barisan Nasional candidate in the Kemaman parliamentary by-election in 2023.
The analysts said any civil servant could join politics once they left public service, while the reality is that even police officers and armed forces generals would have their political preferences like every other Malaysian.
What is more important is not allowing their political leanings to interfere with the execution of their duties, they said
“Even as an academic, I would surely have political leanings. We all have to vote for one party in elections after all. But as long as I don’t let it influence the carrying out of my professional responsibilities, there should be no issue,” said Mazlan.
Azmi, however, acknowledged that people may pay special attention to former senior police officers joining politics as some of them may have been involved in “sensitive” matters while in the force, especially if they were in the Special Branch.
‘Politics not for me’
Separately, former inspector-general of police Khalid Abu Bakar said there was nothing legally or even morally wrong with retired cops going into politics, adding that this was “up to them”.
However, Khalid said he personally would stay away from the political arena. “I just don’t like politics.”
