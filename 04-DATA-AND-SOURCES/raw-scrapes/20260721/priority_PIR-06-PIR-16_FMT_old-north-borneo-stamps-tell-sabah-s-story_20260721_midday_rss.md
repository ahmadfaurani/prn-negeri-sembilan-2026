# Old North Borneo stamps tell Sabah’s story

More than just postage, these century-old North Borneo stamps preserve snapshots of Sabah’s past, documenting its people, wildlife and changing identity. (Bernama pic)
KOTA KINABALU: Long before emails, text messages and social media, postage stamps carried letters across continents.
Today, some of those same stamps have become miniature time capsules, preserving snapshots of Sabah’s history that collectors fear could one day be forgotten.
Among them are North Borneo stamps issued between 1883 and 1963 – rare pieces of postal history that depict everything from indigenous communities and wildlife to logging, sailing boats and colonial rule.
As interest in stamp collecting declines, enthusiasts say preserving these century-old artefacts has become more important than ever.
For Shari Jeffri, founder and president of the Borneo History Association, every stamp tells a story. He describes the collection as a “living archive” that deserves to be passed on to future generations.
“Today’s generation is less exposed to stamp collecting than in the past. Now only a few are really interested, and the number of enthusiasts is also decreasing,” he told Bernama.
His fascination with philately began when he was seven, inspired by a collection inherited from his grandfather, who worked at the Recreation Club Jesselton in the 1920s.
Watching British officers pursue the hobby sparked his grandfather’s own interest. Decades later, those carefully preserved albums ignited Shari’s lifelong passion for researching North Borneo’s postal history.
Among his prized possessions are two 2-cent North Borneo stamps from the original 1883 issue, featuring a brown sailing boat and complete postal cancellations.
To collectors, the postmarks are just as valuable as the stamps themselves, recording when and where a letter began its journey.
Shari Jeffri says preserving North Borneo stamps will help ensure Sabah’s history continues to be passed on to future generations. (Bernama pic)
Over the decades, North Borneo’s stamps evolved alongside the territory.
Early designs featured lions, boats and tigers together with the Latin motto “Pergo et Perago” (“I undertake and I achieve”), while later issues showcased Borneo’s rich flora, fauna and local communities.
A Bernama survey of antique shops in Kota Kinabalu found the stamps are becoming increasingly difficult to find, with prices varying according to their age, rarity and condition.
Among the discoveries was an album containing a six-cent stamp featuring Queen Elizabeth II alongside a Dusun woman, as well as a 10-cent stamp depicting Sabah’s once-thriving logging industry.
Shari says preserving the stamps is about far more than maintaining a collection. “They’re more than a way of paying postage,” he said, “they’re a storyline that records the events of a particular era.”
